SEVERSK / TOMSK-7

Seversk (Russian: Сeверск)

Foundation and abandon: [1949 ; ----]

Location: Tomsl oblast, south middle Russia Coordinate: 56.618690772424166, 84.88458239844809

Basic infos

Established under the cover name “Pyaty Pochtovy” (Fifth Postal) in 1949 to serve the Soviet nuclear weapons complex. Renamed “Tomsk-7,” reflecting its postal code rather than its true identity. During this period the city was omitted from most maps and travel routes, accessible only via special permits. The facility was built in 1954, it housed uranium enrichment facilities, plutonium-producing graphite reactors, and reprocessing plants that supplied fissile material for the USSR’s nuclear arsenal. Seversk operated as one of the USSR’s most tightly guarded sites. Residents surrendered special passes at checkpoints, and discussion of one’s workplace or city of residence was forbidden. Only in 1987 were some travel restrictions eased for study or work in nearby Tomsk. In 1992 President Boris Yeltsin’s decree allowed the closed city to reclaim historical names, and then, Tomsk-7 became Seversk and was officially placed on maps for the first time. On 6 April 1993, a processing tank at the Tomsk-7 reprocessing complex exploded during plutonium-uranium separation. The blast scattered radioactive material over an area up to 28 km away, marking one of the world’s worst post-Chernobyl nuclear accidents (IAEA Level 3 “serious incident”) (just an average Russian day). In 2003, Russia signed to shut down the nuclear facilities, and the two plutonium producing reactors at Seversk were permanently closed by 2008. However... the "Siberian Chemical Combine" continues to operate uranium enrichment and nuclear fuel processing. Seversk remains a closed city, with six checkpoints and entry by permit only. And the recent floodings raised concerns on the radioactive wastes containers intergrity, and could contaminate wider region if another incident occurs.
